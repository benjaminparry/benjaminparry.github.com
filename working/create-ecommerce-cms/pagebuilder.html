Defining The Problem

After the release of Create’s first generation responsive templates, the next challange was to bring the user’s content up to similar standards.

Up until this point content layout was achieved via an integrated WYSIWYG editor, in this case TinyMCE. Similar to the preceding fixed-width templates, TinyMCE used HTML tables for layout. The resulting HTML output was inconsitent from page to page and completely unworkable from a front-end responsive reflow viewpoint.

Owning The Baggage

The project came with baggage: a fairly feature-rich POC that had been delivered by the development team prior to my starting. With it, a key assumption about the primary interaction design i.e. drag and drop.

Althought most of the POC baggage was easily set aside by managing expectations of the team; accepting this as a design but maybe not the design, listening to the learnings from building the POC. However, the drag and drop requirement reamined.

Part of the driving force for this requirement was customer requests and comparisons to competitor solutions. Competitor analysis confirmed drag and drop to be on trend, but would it be right for our customers?

Learning: Test the highest risk assumption first

A small amount of research and/or paper prototype would help to validate whether a drag and drop interaction would best suit our customers would have been more valuble at this stage of the project.
